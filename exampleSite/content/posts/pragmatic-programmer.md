I recently finished re-reading *The Pragmatic Programmer* by Andrew Hunt and David Thomas. Here are my thoughts.

## Why Read It

This book has been a cornerstone of software engineering literature since 1999. The 20th anniversary edition adds modern insights while keeping the timeless advice.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. Care About Your Craft

> "The greatest of all weaknesses is the fear of appearing weak."

Take pride in your work. Write code that you'd be happy to show others.

### 2. DRY — Don't Repeat Yourself

Every piece of knowledge should have a single, unambiguous representation in a system. This is NOT the same as avoiding code duplication.

### 3. The Broken Window Theory

Don't leave "broken windows" (bad designs, wrong decisions, poor code) unrepaired. Fix each one as soon as it's discovered.

## Practical Advice

<!--more-->

### Learn a Text Editor

Master one editor and use it for everything. The authors recommend learning it thoroughly — invest time in configuration, shortcuts, and plugins.

### Use Version Control

This was radical advice in 1999. Today it's standard practice, but the principle remains: version control is your safety net for every project.

### Test Your Software

- Test early, test often
- Automated tests are essential
- Testing is not just QA's job

## What I Liked

The book is full of practical wisdom delivered through memorable analogies. The stone soup story, the boiled frog, the broken window — these metaphors stick with you long after reading.

## What I Didn't Like

Some sections feel dated, especially around specific tools mentioned. But the core principles remain relevant.

## Rating

**5/5** — Essential reading for every developer, from intern to CTO.
